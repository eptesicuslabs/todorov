# David Attwell and Simon Laughlin

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

## David Attwell (b. 1955)

british neuroscientist. Jodrell Professor of Physiology at University College London (UCL). fellow of the Royal Society (1999). fellow of the Academy of Medical Sciences.

attwell's research focuses on the cellular and molecular mechanisms of brain energy supply and demand. he established the first quantitative energy budget for neural signaling, connecting the biophysics of ion channels and synapses to whole-brain metabolic measurements.

key contributions:
- the brain energy budget (attwell and laughlin 2001): quantified energy expenditure on action potentials (47%), postsynaptic potentials (34%), resting potentials (13%), and presynaptic processes (5%) in cortical grey matter
- updated energy budgets with howarth and gleeson (2012): revised action potential costs downward after discovering mammalian neurons are 2-4x more efficient than squid axons
- synaptic energy use and supply (harris, jolivet and attwell 2012): detailed analysis of pre- and postsynaptic energy costs, establishing ~10 fJ per synapse per spike
- non-signalling energy use (engl and attwell 2015): quantified the ~25% of brain energy spent on housekeeping (protein synthesis, organelle transport, membrane maintenance)
- neurovascular coupling: how neural activity regulates local blood flow to match metabolic demand

## Simon Laughlin (b. 1948)

british neuroscientist. professor emeritus at the University of Cambridge, department of zoology. fellow of the Royal Society (2000).

laughlin is one of the founders of the energy-efficiency approach to understanding neural coding. his experimental work on insect visual systems demonstrated that neural codes are shaped by metabolic constraints, and his theoretical work formalized the information-energy tradeoff.

key contributions:
- histogram equalization in fly LMCs (laughlin 1981): demonstrated that large monopolar cells in the blowfly lamina have contrast-response functions matching the cumulative distribution of natural contrasts, maximizing entropy for neurons with limited dynamic range
- energy as a constraint on neural coding (laughlin 2001): argued that metabolic cost is the primary constraint shaping neural codes, not channel capacity or noise alone
- the brain energy budget (attwell and laughlin 2001): the landmark quantification of where the brain's energy goes
- energy limitation and sensory evolution (niven and laughlin 2008): showed that energy limitation is THE selective pressure driving the evolution of sensory system morphology and coding strategies
- communication in neuronal networks (laughlin and sejnowski 2003): analyzed the energy cost of long-range neural communication and its implications for brain wiring

## the attwell and laughlin (2001) paper

"an energy budget for signaling in the grey matter of the brain" (journal of cerebral blood flow and metabolism, 21, 1133-1145) is one of the most cited papers in computational neuroscience.

the paper used anatomic data (neuron density, synapse counts, axon lengths) and physiologic data (ionic currents per spike, release probability, receptor conductances) to build a bottom-up energy budget for cortical signaling. the key finding: neural signaling consumes the majority of brain energy, and the dominant costs are action potentials and postsynaptic receptor currents.

the paper established the framework that all subsequent neural energy analyses build on, including the revised budgets by howarth et al. (2012) and harris et al. (2012).

citation count: >3,000 (as of 2025).
