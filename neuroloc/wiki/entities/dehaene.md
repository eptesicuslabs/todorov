# Stanislas Dehaene

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

Stanislas Dehaene. born January 12, 1965 in Roubaix, France. French cognitive neuroscientist. professor at the College de France, chair of Experimental Cognitive Psychology (since 2005). director of the NeuroSpin neuroimaging center (CEA, Saclay). member of the French Academy of Sciences (2005), the Pontifical Academy of Sciences (2014), and the US National Academy of Sciences (Foreign Associate, 2019).

## education and career

trained in mathematics at the Ecole Normale Superieure, Paris (ENS, 1984). PhD in cognitive science under Jean-Pierre Changeux at the Pasteur Institute and CNRS (1989). postdoctoral work with Michael Posner at the University of Oregon. research director at INSERM (1997-2005). appointed to the College de France in 2005, the youngest professor elected at that time.

## major contributions

### the global neuronal workspace (GNW)

Dehaene's most influential contribution is the neural implementation of [[global_workspace_theory|Baars' Global Workspace Theory]]. with Lionel Naccache (2001) and Jean-Pierre Changeux (2003, 2005, 2011), Dehaene developed the Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) hypothesis, which specifies:
- the neural substrate: long-range cortico-cortical connections linking prefrontal, parietal, and sensory cortices, primarily through large pyramidal neurons in layers II/III and V
- the mechanism: [[ignition_dynamics|ignition]] -- a nonlinear, all-or-none transition from local sensory processing to global broadcast, triggered when a signal exceeds a threshold and activates recurrent feedback loops
- the neural signatures: P3b ERP component (~300 ms), late gamma-band synchrony, sustained prefrontal activation, enhanced long-range synchrony
- the computational model: a network with modular local processors, sparse long-range excitatory connections, and balanced excitation/inhibition that exhibits bistability (ignited vs non-ignited states)

the GNW became one of the two leading scientific theories of consciousness alongside [[integrated_information_theory|IIT]], and was the subject of a major adversarial collaboration funded by the Templeton Foundation (2023-2025).

### conscious access and subliminal processing

Dehaene's experimental program demonstrated that stimuli can be processed extensively without conscious access:
- masked words are processed semantically (priming), phonologically, and even at the motor level, but not reportable (Dehaene et al. 1998, 2001)
- the attentional blink shows that a stimulus can fail to reach consciousness even when it is fully visible, because the workspace is occupied by a previous stimulus
- the "all-or-none" nature of conscious access: near-threshold stimuli show bimodal distributions of neural activity (seen vs unseen), consistent with the ignition model

### the number sense

Dehaene is also known for his work on numerical cognition. "The Number Sense" (Oxford University Press, 1997/2011) summarized his research on how the brain represents numbers. key findings include the distance effect, the SNARC effect (spatial-numerical association), and the identification of the intraparietal sulcus as the neural substrate of approximate number representation. this work is separate from his consciousness research.

### reading and education

Dehaene extended his research to reading ("Reading in the Brain," 2009), identifying the visual word form area (VWFA) in the left fusiform gyrus and proposing the "neuronal recycling" hypothesis: cultural inventions (reading, mathematics) co-opt brain circuits that evolved for other purposes (object recognition, spatial cognition). this work has influenced educational policy in France, where Dehaene served on the Scientific Council of National Education (2018-present).
