# John O'Keefe

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

(b. 1939, New York City, USA)

Sainsbury Wellcome Centre for Neural Circuits and Behaviour, University College London. previously Department of Anatomy, UCL.

## contributions

discovered place cells in the hippocampus (1971), establishing the neural basis of spatial memory and the concept of the "cognitive map." awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2014, shared with May-Britt Moser and Edvard Moser, "for their discoveries of cells that constitute a positioning system in the brain."

key contributions:

- **place cells** (O'Keefe & Dostrovsky, 1971, Brain Research): first report of hippocampal pyramidal neurons that fire at specific spatial locations in freely moving rats. O'Keefe identified these "place units" as encoding the animal's position per se, not any specific sensory input. this was the first evidence of an explicit spatial representation in the brain
- **The Hippocampus as a Cognitive Map** (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978, Oxford University Press): the foundational monograph proposing that the hippocampus implements an allocentric spatial map, with place cells as its fundamental units. built on Tolman's 1948 behavioral evidence for cognitive maps and provided the neural mechanistic account. the book was initially controversial but has become one of the most cited works in systems neuroscience (>8000 citations for the foundational papers)
- **phase precession** (O'Keefe & Recce, 1993, Hippocampus): discovered that the theta phase of place cell spikes shifts systematically as the animal traverses the place field, encoding sub-field position with high precision. this demonstrated that spike timing relative to ongoing oscillations carries spatial information beyond what firing rate provides
- **boundary vector cells**: contributed to the theory of how environmental boundaries shape place fields, connecting place cell firing to the geometry of the environment
- **oscillatory interference model**: co-developed (with Burgess and Barry, 2007) the model proposing that grid cell firing arises from interference between velocity-controlled oscillators, providing a mechanistic account of grid cells based on theta oscillations

## recognition

Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (2014), shared with May-Britt Moser and Edvard Moser. Fellow of the Royal Society (1992). Kavli Prize in Neuroscience (2014). born in New York to Irish immigrant parents, raised in the South Bronx, studied at City College of New York (BA) and McGill University (PhD under Ronald Melzack).
