# Giulio Tononi

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

Giulio Tononi. born 1960 in Trento, Italy. psychiatrist and neuroscientist. David P. White Chair in Sleep Medicine and professor of psychiatry at the University of Wisconsin-Madison (since 2001). founder and director of the Wisconsin Institute for Sleep and Consciousness.

## education and career

medical degree from the University of Padua, Italy. PhD in neuroscience under Lamberto Maffei at the Scuola Normale Superiore, Pisa (1990). postdoctoral work with Gerald Edelman at the Neurosciences Institute, San Diego (1990-2000), where he collaborated on the neural group selection theory (Neural Darwinism) and developed early ideas on consciousness. moved to the University of Wisconsin-Madison in 2001.

## major contributions

### integrated information theory (IIT)

Tononi's central contribution is [[integrated_information_theory]] (IIT), first proposed in 2004. the theory claims that consciousness IS integrated information, measured by Phi. a system is conscious to the degree that it generates information (has many distinguishable states) AND integrates that information (the whole generates more than the sum of its parts). Phi quantifies the irreducible cause-effect structure of a system.

IIT is distinctive among theories of consciousness for starting from phenomenology (the properties of experience) and deriving physical requirements. five axioms about the nature of experience (intrinsicality, information, integration, exclusion, composition) map to five postulates about the physical substrate. the theory has evolved through four major versions:
- IIT 1.0 (2004): original formulation, Phi as minimum information partition
- IIT 2.0 (Balduzzi & Tononi 2008): refined mathematical framework
- IIT 3.0 (Oizumi, Albantakis & Tononi 2014): introduced Phi-structure (qualia space), five axioms/postulates
- IIT 4.0 (Albantakis et al. 2023): reformulated using "operational physicalism"

### key predictions of IIT

- feedforward networks have Phi = 0 and are never conscious, regardless of computational sophistication
- the cerebellum (despite having more neurons than cerebral cortex) contributes minimally to consciousness due to its feedforward-dominated, modular architecture
- consciousness resides primarily in posterior cortex (the "posterior hot zone"), not prefrontal cortex as GWT predicts
- consciousness is graded and widespread (panpsychism): any system with Phi > 0 has some degree of consciousness

### the adversarial collaboration (2023-2025)

Tononi participated in a major empirical test funded by the Templeton Foundation, pitting IIT against the [[global_workspace_theory|Global Neuronal Workspace]]. participants viewed images while undergoing fMRI and EEG. IIT's predictions about sustained posterior cortical activity were partially confirmed (2 of 3 pre-registered predictions). GWT's predictions about prefrontal involvement were not confirmed (0 of 3). the results were published in Nature (2025) and remain contested.

### sleep research

Tononi has made major contributions to sleep science, proposing the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis (SHY, Tononi & Cirelli 2003, 2006): waking experience produces net synaptic potentiation throughout the brain, and sleep serves to renormalize synaptic strength through downscaling. this explains why sleep deprivation impairs cognitive function (synaptic saturation) and why sleep is restorative (synaptic renormalization). SHY has been partially supported by molecular, electrophysiological, and structural evidence.

### the perturbational complexity index (PCI)

with Marcello Massimini, Tononi developed the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI, Casali et al. 2013): a measure of brain complexity derived from the EEG response to transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS). PCI quantifies the information content of the cortical response -- how differentiated and integrated the brain's reaction is. high PCI (~0.3-0.6) during waking and REM sleep, low PCI (~0.1-0.2) during non-REM sleep, anesthesia, and vegetative state. PCI has been validated as a reliable diagnostic tool for disorders of consciousness and is IIT-inspired: it measures how much integrated information the brain produces in response to perturbation.

## criticisms received

IIT has attracted significant criticism:
- computational intractability: Phi cannot be computed for systems larger than ~12-15 elements (Barrett & Seth 2011)
- panpsychism: simple physical systems have nonzero Phi, implying consciousness in XOR gate arrays (Aaronson 2014)
- unfalsifiability: consciousness = Phi is an identity claim that cannot be independently verified (Cerullo 2015)
- the adversarial collaboration results are contested on methodological grounds

Tononi has responded to each criticism within the IIT framework, particularly in the IIT 4.0 formulation which addresses the causal structure concerns raised by Aaronson.
