# Karl Friston

Karl John Friston FRS FMedSci FRSB. born July 12, 1959. British neuroscientist and theoretician at University College London. scientific director of the Wellcome Trust Centre for Neuroimaging (2001-present). ranked #1 on Semantic Scholar's list of most influential neuroscientists (2016). Google Scholar h-index: 291.

## education and early career

studied natural sciences at Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge (1980). medical degree from King's College Medical School, London. specialist training in psychiatry at Oxford University (1988). research stay at Hammersmith Hospital neuroimaging department. researcher at the Institute of Neurology, UCL, since 1994.

## major contributions

### statistical parametric mapping (SPM)

Friston invented SPM in the early 1990s, providing the first rigorous statistical framework for analyzing functional neuroimaging data (PET, fMRI). SPM constructs statistical images (parametric maps) from voxel-wise hypothesis tests, enabling researchers to identify brain regions that activate during cognitive tasks. the SPM software package became the dominant tool in neuroimaging, used by thousands of laboratories worldwide. this work alone would secure Friston's legacy as one of the most impactful neuroscientists.

### voxel-based morphometry (VBM)

VBM (Ashburner & Friston, 2000) allows automated whole-brain comparison of local gray matter volume between groups. it became the standard method for structural neuroimaging studies of neurodegenerative disease, psychiatry, and development.

### dynamic causal modelling (DCM)

DCM (Friston et al., 2003) is a framework for inferring directed connectivity between brain regions from neuroimaging data. unlike functional connectivity (which measures correlations), DCM models causal interactions by fitting a generative model of neural dynamics to observed data. it uses Bayesian model comparison to adjudicate between competing hypotheses about brain connectivity.

### the free energy principle

the [[free_energy_principle]] (Friston 2005, 2006, 2010) is the proposal that all adaptive systems minimize variational free energy -- an upper bound on surprise. this subsumes [[predictive_coding]] as a special case and extends it with active inference (actions that reduce expected surprise). the principle claims to provide a unified account of perception, action, learning, attention, and emotion under a single information-theoretic objective.

the free energy principle is Friston's most ambitious and most contested contribution. it has generated thousands of citations and an active research community (active inference, Markov blankets, the Bayesian brain), but it has also attracted criticism for unfalsifiability (Andrews 2021), overreach (Colombo & Wright 2021), and mathematical opacity.

### active inference

active inference (Friston et al., 2017) extends the free energy principle from perception (updating beliefs) to action (changing the world). an organism does not maximize reward; it minimizes expected free energy by acting to fulfill its prior expectations about what sensory states it should observe. this framework reinterprets reinforcement learning, optimal control, and planning as special cases of free energy minimization.
