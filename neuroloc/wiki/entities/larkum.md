# Matthew Larkum

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

## biographical summary

Matthew E. Larkum (b. ~1968, Humboldt University of Berlin, formerly University of Bern) is a neuroscientist whose work on apical amplification and BAC firing in layer 5 pyramidal neurons has provided a cellular mechanism for cortical associations. his research connects single-neuron dendritic physiology to large-scale theories of cortical computation, consciousness, and predictive coding.

## the BAC firing discovery (1999)

Larkum, Zhu, and Sakmann (1999, Nature) discovered that L5 pyramidal neurons have two distinct integration zones: the somatic zone (receiving feedforward input) and the calcium spike initiation zone (CaIZ, at the top of the apical trunk, receiving feedback input). when a backpropagating action potential from the soma coincides with depolarization at the CaIZ from apical tuft input, a dendritic calcium spike is triggered that propagates back to the soma and causes a burst of action potentials. this backpropagation-activated calcium spike (BAC) firing requires coincident activation of both zones and is the first demonstrated cellular mechanism for associating inputs arriving at different cortical layers.

## the cortical associations paper (2013)

Larkum (2013, Trends in Neurosciences) proposed that BAC firing is "an organizing principle for the cerebral cortex." the argument:

1. L5 pyramidal neurons span the cortical column, with basal dendrites in deep layers (receiving feedforward input) and apical tuft in L1 (receiving feedback from higher areas and nonspecific thalamus).
2. BAC firing detects coincidence between feedforward evidence and feedback context/predictions.
3. this provides a cellular substrate for cortical associations: sensory features are linked to context when both streams are active simultaneously.
4. the mechanism is tightly controlled by inhibitory interneurons (SST+ targeting apical dendrites), allowing the cortical circuit to gate which associations are formed.

Larkum distinguished two modes: apical amplification (top-down input amplifies response to bottom-up input, normal perception) and apical drive (top-down input alone triggers a burst, mental imagery/hallucination).

## anesthesia and consciousness (2020)

Suzuki and Larkum (2020, Cell) showed that general anesthesia selectively decouples the two integration zones of L5 pyramidal neurons. under anesthesia, somatic action potentials still occur (feedforward processing preserved) but BAC firing is abolished (top-down modulation eliminated). this provides a cellular mechanism for loss of consciousness under anesthesia: feedforward sensory processing continues, but the integration of sensory evidence with top-down context is blocked.

## relevance to todorov

Larkum's work establishes that the L5 pyramidal neuron is a two-input coincidence detector, not a single-input integrator. todorov's SwiGLU receives only one input (the residual stream), making it a "single-input" device. the BAC firing framework predicts that adding a second input source (top-down context or feedback) to SwiGLU's gating mechanism could improve context-dependent processing. see [[apical_amplification]] and [[dendritic_computation_to_swiglu]].

the anesthesia result (Suzuki & Larkum 2020) provides an interesting lens: a purely feedforward network without feedback connections is "anesthetized" in the Larkum sense -- it processes input without contextual integration.
