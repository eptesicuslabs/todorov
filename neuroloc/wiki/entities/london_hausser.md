# Michael London and Michael Hausser

## biographical summary

Michael London (Hebrew University of Jerusalem) and Michael Hausser (University College London) are neuroscientists whose 2005 review "Dendritic Computation" in the Annual Review of Neuroscience established the modern framework for understanding dendrites as active computational elements.

## Michael Hausser

Michael Hausser (b. 1965, Oxford-trained, now at UCL Wolfson Institute for Biomedical Research) pioneered the use of patch-clamp electrophysiology for recording directly from dendrites. his laboratory developed methods for simultaneous soma-dendrite recording, two-photon calcium imaging of dendritic activity, and all-optical interrogation of neural circuits using holographic optogenetics.

key contributions beyond the 2005 review:
- co-developed dendritic patch-clamp recording techniques with Bert Sakmann
- demonstrated that action potentials actively backpropagate into dendrites (with Greg Stuart)
- showed that individual synaptic inputs can be detected and manipulated optically
- founded the UCL Neuroscience domain and the Sainsbury Wellcome Centre for Neural Circuits and Behaviour (co-founder)
- Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS, 2015)
- Wellcome Trust Principal Research Fellow

## Michael London

Michael London (Hebrew University of Jerusalem, Edmond and Lily Safra Center for Brain Sciences) studies computation in single neurons and neural circuits. his work uses a combination of electrophysiology, modeling, and theory to understand how neurons process information.

key contributions:
- the 2005 review systematically catalogued dendritic computational mechanisms (supralinear summation, sublinear summation, coincidence detection, directional selectivity, compartmentalization)
- theoretical work on information processing capacity of single neurons
- computational models of dendritic integration

## the 2005 review

the London and Hausser (2005) review argued that:
- dendrites implement a range of linear and nonlinear computations
- the contribution of single neurons to brain computation has been underestimated
- dendrites are not passive cables but active processors with local spike generation
- a pyramidal neuron should be understood as a multi-compartment computer, not a point integrator

over 2,500 citations. the review is the standard reference for dendritic computation as a field.

## relevance to todorov

the London and Hausser framework establishes that neurons are more computationally powerful than point models (like ATMN, LIF) suggest. the multiplicative gating in SwiGLU captures one aspect of dendritic computation (coincidence detection) but misses the multi-branch independence that gives biological neurons their expanded capacity. see [[dendritic_computation]] and [[dendritic_computation_to_swiglu]].
