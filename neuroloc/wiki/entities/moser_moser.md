# May-Britt Moser and Edvard Moser

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

May-Britt Moser (b. 1963, Fosnavag, Norway) and Edvard Ingjald Moser (b. 1962, Alesund, Norway)

Kavli Institute for Systems Neuroscience and Centre for Neural Computation, Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU), Trondheim.

## contributions

discovered grid cells in the medial entorhinal cortex (2005), revealing the brain's coordinate system for spatial navigation. awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2014, shared with John O'Keefe, "for their discoveries of cells that constitute a positioning system in the brain." they are the only married couple to share a Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (they divorced in 2016 but continue to collaborate).

key contributions:

- **grid cells** (Hafting, Fyhn, Molden, Moser & Moser, 2005, Nature): discovered neurons in the medial entorhinal cortex (MEC) that fire in a regular hexagonal lattice pattern as rats move through space. each grid cell is characterized by a specific spacing, orientation, and phase. the hexagonal firing pattern was one of the most geometrically precise structures ever found in the mammalian brain. this established that a coordinate system for space exists upstream of the hippocampus, providing the metric input to the hippocampal place cell map
- **modular organization of grid cells** (Stensola et al., 2012, Nature): demonstrated that grid cells are organized into discrete modules with geometrically scaled spacings (ratio ~1.4-1.7x between successive modules). modules maintain internal coherence during remapping, operating as independent functional units. this discretization has profound information-theoretic implications: the combined code across modules can represent positions with exponential precision
- **border cells** (Solstad et al., 2008, Science): identified MEC neurons that fire along environmental boundaries, providing geometric constraints for the spatial representation and error correction for path integration
- **speed cells** (Kropff, Carmichael, Moser & Moser, 2015, Nature): discovered MEC neurons whose firing rate increases linearly with running speed, providing the speed signal needed for path integration. speed cells are functionally distinct from grid cells, head direction cells, and border cells
- **toroidal topology** (Gardner et al., 2022, Nature): used Neuropixels recordings and topological data analysis to demonstrate that the population activity of grid cell modules lives on a toroidal manifold, confirming a key prediction of continuous attractor network models
- **spatial representation in the entorhinal cortex**: extensive body of work characterizing the full range of spatial cell types in MEC, including head direction cells, conjunctive grid x head direction cells, and object-vector cells

## recognition

Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (2014), shared with John O'Keefe. Kavli Prize in Neuroscience (2014). both are members of the Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters and the Royal Norwegian Society of Sciences and Letters. Edvard Moser is a foreign member of the US National Academy of Sciences. May-Britt Moser is a member of EMBO. both studied psychology at the University of Oslo and trained as postdoctoral researchers with O'Keefe at UCL before establishing their independent lab in Trondheim. they founded the Kavli Institute for Systems Neuroscience and the Centre for Neural Computation at NTNU.
