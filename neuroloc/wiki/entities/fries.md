# Pascal Fries

status: definitional. last fact-checked 2026-04-16.

(b. 1969, Germany)

director, Ernst Strungmann Institute (ESI) for Neuroscience, Frankfurt. professor, Radboud University Nijmegen (previously).

## contributions

developed the communication through coherence (CTC) hypothesis, the most influential theoretical framework for how neural oscillations mediate selective communication between brain areas.

key contributions:

- **communication through coherence** (Fries, 2005, Trends in Cognitive Sciences; Fries, 2015, Neuron): proposed that neuronal communication is gated by rhythmic synchronization. two populations communicate effectively only when their gamma oscillations are coherent (aligned in phase). attention selects information by adjusting which populations become coherent with downstream areas. the 2015 update integrated gamma (feedforward), alpha-beta (feedback), and theta (attentional sampling) into a unified multi-frequency framework
- **attentional modulation of gamma synchronization** (Fries et al., 2001, Science): demonstrated in macaque V4 that selective attention increases gamma-band synchronization among neurons representing the attended stimulus while decreasing synchronization for the unattended stimulus. foundational experimental evidence for CTC
- **modulation of neuronal interactions through synchronization** (Womelsdorf et al., 2007, Science): showed that the phase relationship between oscillations in parietal and temporal cortex predicts effective connectivity, with phase relations supporting interactions PRECEDING those interactions by milliseconds -- consistent with a causal role for synchrony
- **attentional stimulus selection through selective synchronization** (Bosman et al., 2012, Neuron): demonstrated that V1-V4 gamma coherence is selectively enhanced for attended stimuli, with the V4 gamma rhythm entraining specifically to the attended V1 representation
- **theta-rhythmic attentional sampling** (Landau and Fries, 2012, Current Biology): showed that visual attention samples competing stimuli at ~4 Hz (theta), alternating between locations on successive theta cycles
