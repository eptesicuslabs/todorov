# canonical cortical microcircuit simulation

## what this does

canonical_circuit.py builds a simplified canonical microcircuit in Brian2:

1. **excitatory population** (400 LIF neurons): each neuron has a preferred orientation (uniformly spaced from 0 to 180 degrees). recurrent excitatory connections are orientation-tuned: neurons with similar preferred orientations have stronger connections (gaussian tuning with sigma = 30 degrees). this implements the like-to-like connectivity of the Douglas and Martin canonical microcircuit.

2. **inhibitory population** (100 LIF neurons): fast-spiking (tau_m = 10 ms, shorter refractory period). receive broad excitatory input from the excitatory population. provide untuned (broad) feedback inhibition to excitatory neurons. this implements the PV+ basket cell population that provides gain control.

3. **thalamic input** (50 Poisson neurons): represent orientation-tuned thalamic drive. during stimulation, they fire at 200 Hz. the connection weights to excitatory neurons are tuned: neurons whose preferred orientation matches the stimulus orientation (45 degrees) receive stronger input.

the simulation runs three phases:
- spontaneous activity (200 ms): no thalamic input, only background noise
- stimulation (500 ms): thalamic input at 45 degrees orientation
- post-stimulus (200 ms): thalamic input off

## outputs

canonical_circuit.png contains four panels:

1. **raster plot**: spike times of all 500 neurons (excitatory in blue, inhibitory in red). during stimulation, excitatory neurons near the stimulus orientation fire more densely.

2. **population firing rates**: smoothed firing rates of excitatory and inhibitory populations. both increase during stimulation (E/I balance tracks input), with the excitatory rate modulated by orientation tuning.

3. **orientation tuning curve**: firing rate as a function of preferred orientation during stimulation vs spontaneous activity. the peak at the stimulus orientation (45 degrees) demonstrates orientation selectivity generated by recurrent amplification.

4. **recurrent amplification**: normalized comparison of thalamic input strength vs cortical response. the cortical response is sharper (narrower tuning width) than the thalamic input, demonstrating the iceberg effect: recurrent amplification combined with inhibitory thresholding produces sharper selectivity than the feedforward input alone.


## what to look for

the key demonstration is in panel 4: the cortical response (blue solid) should be narrower than the thalamic input (green dashed). this is the signature of recurrent amplification in the canonical microcircuit:

- the thalamic input provides a BROAD orientation bias (gaussian with sigma = 45 degrees)
- recurrent excitatory connections amplify the response of neurons near the peak orientation
- untuned inhibition (the iceberg effect) suppresses responses at non-preferred orientations
- the result: sharper orientation tuning in the cortical output than in the thalamic input

the spontaneous firing rate (gray bars in panel 3) should be low and approximately uniform across orientations, confirming that the orientation selectivity is stimulus-driven, not a bias in the network.

## limitations

- this is a single-layer model. the real canonical microcircuit involves interactions between L4 (input), L2/3 (recurrent amplification), and multiple inhibitory subtypes (PV+, SST+, VIP+). this simulation collapses all excitatory neurons into one population.
- the connection probabilities and weights are hand-tuned, not derived from anatomical data. the Potjans and Diesmann (2014) model provides quantitative connectivity, but simulating it requires ~80,000 neurons and is much more computationally expensive.
- orientation tuning is assigned, not learned. in the real cortex, orientation selectivity emerges from the arrangement of ON/OFF thalamic inputs and is refined by experience.
- the model uses LIF neurons. real cortical neurons have adaptation, bursting, and subthreshold oscillations that affect the dynamics of the canonical circuit.

## connection to todorov

see [[cortical_microcircuit_to_layer_schedule]] for the bridge analysis. the simulation demonstrates a property that todorov's architecture does NOT have: selective amplification of specific input features through recurrent connectivity with like-to-like bias. todorov's KDA layers perform delta-rule recurrence (associative memory), which amplifies patterns stored in the recurrent state, but the amplification is not orientation-tuned or feature-selective in the cortical sense. the analogy is structural (both use recurrence for amplification) but mechanistically distinct.
